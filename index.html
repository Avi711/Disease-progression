Motivation

Why search logs can reveal illness timelines

Context for CS readers

Medical Background

Three concepts you need to read the search data correctly.

1

Tumor & Biopsy

A tumor is an abnormal mass: benign means non-cancerous; malignant means cancerous. A biopsy checks which one it is.

2

Diagnosis & Staging

Pathology confirms the type, grade, and stage. Stages run from 0 (non-invasive) to IV (metastasized to other organs).

3

Treatment Pipeline

Care follows a typical order: surgery (lumpectomy / mastectomy) → chemotherapy and / or radiation.

Why this matters: each of these events leaves a distinctive search fingerprint — and they appear in a predictable order.
Introduction

The Core Algorithmic Problem

The reality of diagnosis

A cancer diagnosis is a highly disruptive event — patients are immersed in new terminology, financial concerns, and time-sensitive decisions.

The algorithmic challenge

Search logs are extremely noisy. How do we tell apart…

  • a searcher consistent with a new diagnosis
  • a medical student studying the disease
  • someone who has had the illness for years

The goals

  1. DX
    Searcher classification
    A probabilistic classifier that identifies users actively experiencing a new diagnosis.
  2. DDX
    Timeline classification
    A model that estimates the day the diagnosis was learned.
  3. HMM
    Episode discovery
    Align inferred timelines to find standard patterns of information-seeking across the illness.

Date conflict resolution

Large disagreement

DDX dates that differed by more than 7 days were discussed and resolved by the annotators.

Small disagreement

When annotators were within 1-7 days, the paper chose the later day, because pathology confirmation often follows the first suspicious signal.

Why the lower agreement?

There is only one true positive day per user history, so exact-day labeling is much more fragile than deciding whether the user is PP.

Models

Predict who, then when

The model

Core algorithm — MART

Multiple Additive Regression Trees — gradient-boosted trees.

How it works

A sequence of small decision trees — each new tree learns to predict what the previous trees got wrong. Sum their outputs and you get one strong predictor from many weak ones.

Why use it here

  • Interpretability — feature importance is inspectable
  • Robust against noisy labels (which we have!)
  • Robust against missing values
  • Strong out-of-the-box performance on tabular features

The model · timeline

DDX — coarse-to-fine day prediction

Three classifiers, multiplied, to pinpoint a single day in months of noise.

Step 1

Window classifier

Coarse: predict a 15-day window around the diagnosis.

×
Step 2

Day classifier

Fine: predict the exact day, strictly inside that window.

×
Step 3

State transition

For any day, predict whether the user has been diagnosed yet.

Joint: P(day = d) = Pwindow(d) · Pday(d) · Pstate(d)
Also uses before-and-after features — the ratio of query activity before vs after each candidate day.
For the curious · the paper's actual formula

DDX — the full probability function

The three classifiers
θAD
Around Diagnosis

For day d: probability d falls inside a 15-day window around the diagnosis.

θWW
Within Window

Conditional on d being in the window: probability d is the exact diagnosis day.

θHB
Has Been diagnosed

For any day, predict label 0 (not yet) vs 1 (already) — a step function.

Combined prediction rule
P(d) ∝ P(d ∈ window | θAD)
· P(d = 1 | d ∈ window, θWW)
· P(d−1 = 0 | θHB)
· P(d+1 = 1 | θHB)
Equation 2 gives the coarse-to-fine AD/WW term; the HB terms are multiplied afterward:
P(d ∈ window | θAD) = Σi=d−14..d P(win = {i, …, i+14})
In plain English: for each candidate day d, ask all three classifiers — "is d in a window?", "is d the day, given the window?", "did the step from 0→1 happen at d?" — and multiply the probabilities. Pick the d with the highest joint score.

if experiential_ratio > τ → positive
DDX baseline

Heuristic day pick

Estimate the day of diagnosis by simply picking either:

  • the first day with experiential terms, or
  • the day with the maximum volume of experiential queries.

Episode discovery

What changes after timelines are aligned?

Once histories are aligned to inferred DDX, the paper studies phases of attention over time.

Episode discovery

A block HMM learns the clinical sequence

A block HMM = a regular HMM, but each day emits a bag of ontology categories instead of a single symbol. The model uses 5 states; the DDX state is fixed to each user's inferred diagnosis day.

On 558 high-confidence timelines, the constrained HMM recovers a sequence consistent with the clinical story: pre-DDX → DDX → branching into immediate surgery vs long-term chemo. Background emissions are isolated so clinical transitions stay clean.
From the paper · Fig. 1

The HMM the model actually discovered

Real transition probabilities and top-emitted terms per state.

Strong self-loops (e.g. 0.66, 0.79, 0.83) — once a user enters a stage they tend to stay there. After DDX, the model branches into immediate care (mastectomy / radiation / lumpectomy) and longer-term treatment (chemotherapy / hair loss / drugs).

Clinical reality check

The order matches clinical care, but search pivots come earlier: workup→DDX averages 20 d vs a 29 d mammogram-to-diagnosis median; surgery and chemo searches also precede typical procedure timing.

The disruption

Non-cancer queries drop to 0.88× baseline at 60–90 d after DDX and 0.79× at 300–330 d — cancer search cuts into normal life. Cancer-related search is 3.89× heavier 60–90 d after DDX than before, and still 1.25× baseline a year later.

Aligned queries around the diagnosis day

High-confidence timelines aligned so day 0 is the inferred DDX; counts thin out far from the pivot days.

A pivot point = the first day a user searches for terms from a clinical phase — screening (first mammography/ultrasound query before DDX), surgery (first surgery query after DDX), chemo (first chemo query after the surgery pivot). The average reveals the clinical sequence, but not every user contributes every pivot or every day.

06
Limits and takeaways

What should we trust, and what should we question?

The final section separates useful aggregate insight from individual-level uncertainty.

Responsibility

Ethics, privacy & data handling

01

Consent & anonymization

Data from users who explicitly consented via a browser add-on’s terms. All user-identifying information stripped at the source.

02

IP scrubbing

Researchers had no IP addresses. Location aggregated to city / town level — one lat-long for the whole town — to prevent individual tracking.

03

No direct contact

Researchers could not contact users to verify diagnoses — which is exactly why epidemiological validation methods were needed.

What the authors do not claim

Limitations & framing

×
Not verified medical truth. Labels are inferred from search behavior on a manually annotated subset.
×
Not “these users are definitely patients.” The authors carefully avoid this claim.
✓
The actual claim: we identify searchers exhibiting a strong, sudden interest in breast cancer amid a larger temporal structure that mimics the clinical sequence of a patient.
!
The “loved one” confound. Diagnosis triggers intense search by family too — the algorithm likely captures whole care networks, not strictly patients.
Wrapping up

Conclusion & CS implications

📜 Summary

Large-scale anonymized search logs contain temporal signals that align with clinical episodes of serious illness.

⚙️ Implication for system design

By predicting a user’s current episode, search engines can dynamically tailor results — surfacing “treatment preparation” info instead of “diagnosis explainers” for someone weeks into care.

💡 Broader impact

A new framework for understanding evolving, day-to-day health information gaps — paving the way for smarter decision-support systems.
